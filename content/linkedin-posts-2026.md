## 2026-01-23 — From POC to Production: LLM Workflows at Scale

**Status:** published · **Topic:** AI / Production

From POC to Production: How to Actually Run LLM Workflows at Scale

Your RAG prototype works. Your agent demo impresses stakeholders. But can it handle Monday morning traffic when 1,000 users hit it simultaneously?

Most teams nail the AI part. They miss the operations part.

The Three Pillars of Operational LLMs:

1. Observability That Actually Works

Your LLM pipeline has 5-10 steps: retrieval, chunking, embedding, vector search, prompt assembly, model calls, post-processing. When something breaks, which step failed?

Log every step with timestamps and token counts. Track latency at each stage (retrieval is often the bottleneck). Monitor cost per query (it varies wildly with context size). Alert on error rates, not just downtime.

Without this, you're debugging blind. Production issues take days instead of hours.

2. Reliability Patterns

LLMs fail in ways traditional APIs don't: rate limits, timeouts, quality degradation, cost explosions.

What actually works: retry with exponential backoff (cap retries to prevent runaway costs), fallback models, circuit breakers, response caching, prompt versioning.

3. Data Pipeline Discipline

RAG systems are only as good as your data pipeline. Chunking strategy, embedding freshness, source attribution, quality monitoring. Most teams build the RAG query path first. The ingestion pipeline becomes an afterthought, and it shows.

The hard truth: building the AI is 30% of the work. Making it reliable, observable, and cost-effective in production is the other 70%.

Before you ship that agent workflow to customers, ask: What happens when the vector DB is slow? How do you debug a bad answer? What's your cost per query at 10x current volume? Can you roll back a prompt change without redeploying?

If you can't answer these, you're not ready for production.

## 2026-01-20 — What Production-First AI Actually Means

**Status:** draft · **Topic:** AI Readiness

Most AI projects start by optimizing the model. We start by optimizing for production.

The difference matters more than you'd think.

Model-first teams chase benchmark scores. They fine-tune for weeks. They build elaborate evaluation harnesses. Then they try to deploy and discover that their model is too slow, too expensive, or too fragile for real users.

Production-first means asking different questions from day one:

What's the acceptable latency for this user interaction? That constrains your model choice and architecture.

What happens when the model gives a bad answer? That drives your fallback logic and human-in-the-loop design.

What does it cost to serve one request? That determines whether your feature has viable unit economics.

How will we measure quality in production? That shapes your observability and evaluation approach.

The model is one component. Production is the whole system: the infrastructure, the monitoring, the cost controls, the user experience, the failure modes.

When you start with production constraints, you make different decisions. Simpler models. Cached responses where appropriate. Tiered processing that uses cheaper models for easy queries. Graceful degradation instead of hard failures.

The result is an AI feature that ships faster, runs reliably, and costs predictably. Not the most technically impressive approach. But the one that actually reaches users.

## 2026-01-17 — Data Quality Is the Real AI Blocker

**Status:** draft · **Topic:** AI Readiness

Nobody wants to hear this, but data quality is the single biggest blocker for AI adoption in B2B SaaS.

Not model selection. Not infrastructure. Not talent. Data quality.

I've seen teams spend months evaluating LLM providers and debating fine-tuning strategies, only to discover their training data has 30% duplicate records and inconsistent field formatting.

Here's what data readiness actually looks like for AI:

Completeness. What percentage of records have all the fields your model needs? If it's below 80%, fix that first.

Consistency. Are the same concepts represented the same way across your system? "United States," "US," "USA," and "U.S.A." might all mean the same thing, but your embedding model doesn't know that.

Freshness. How old is the data your AI will use? If your knowledge base is 6 months out of date, your AI will confidently give wrong answers. That's worse than no AI at all.

Pipeline reliability. It doesn't matter how clean your data is today if tomorrow's ETL job fails silently and your AI starts serving stale results.

The unsexy truth is that the most impactful AI preparation work looks like data engineering, not data science. Clean pipelines, validated schemas, monitored freshness, documented lineage.

It's not the work anyone wants to do. But it's the work that determines whether your AI feature works.

## 2026-01-13 — Build vs. Buy Is the Wrong Question

**Status:** draft · **Topic:** AI Readiness

"Should we build or buy our AI solution?"

I hear this question constantly. And it's the wrong framing.

The right question is: "Which components should we build, which should we buy, and which should we assemble from open source?"

An AI feature isn't a single thing. It's a stack: an LLM provider, an embedding model, a vector database, retrieval logic, prompt management, evaluation framework, cost monitoring, and a user-facing interface.

You might use a managed API for the LLM, an open-source model for embeddings, a managed vector database, and custom-built retrieval and evaluation logic.

The decision for each layer should be based on three factors:

Differentiation value. If this component is where your competitive advantage lives, build it. If it's commodity infrastructure, buy it.

Maintenance burden. Everything you build, you maintain forever. Be honest about what your team has the capacity to operate at 2am.

Lock-in risk. How hard is it to switch later? For commodity layers, prefer options with clean abstractions.

"Build vs. buy" as a binary makes teams either over-build (everything custom) or over-buy (everything vendor). Component-level thinking gives you control where it matters and leverage where it doesn't.

## 2026-01-13 — The Hidden Costs of AI

**Status:** published · **Topic:** AI / FinOps

The Hidden Costs of AI: Why Your POC Budget Won't Cover Production

That impressive AI demo your team built for $500/month using OpenAI or Anthropic? Expect $2-5K/month in production. The API pricing is simple, but the real costs aren't.

Where the Money Actually Goes:

Token Volume. Your POC tested 50 queries/day. Production serves thousands. Tokens scale linearly, but context windows don't stay small.

Embedding and Vector Storage. RAG pipelines mean embedding your corpus, storing vectors, and re-embedding when you update content.

Data Pipeline Overhead. Ingestion, chunking, preprocessing, quality monitoring: the invisible tax on every AI feature.

Prompt Engineering Iterations. That 500-token prompt will become 2,000 tokens once you add guardrails, few-shot examples, and edge case handling.

The Scaling Surprise:

Your POC processes 100 queries/day. Production serves 10,000. That's 100x the API cost, plus retry logic, caching layers, logging and observability, and fallback handling when the API is slow.

Before You Approve the AI Roadmap:

Model your cost per query at 10x and 100x current volume. Factor in data engineering hours (they're 40-60% of total AI project cost). Budget for prompt iteration and context window growth. Plan for API pricing changes (they happen).

The POC proved the technology works. Production proves the unit economics work. Budget accordingly.

## 2026-01-10 — The Cost Model Nobody Built

**Status:** draft · **Topic:** AI Readiness

Here's a number that should scare you: the difference between running an AI feature for 10 users and 10,000 users can be 100x in cost. Not 10x. A hundred.

Most teams I work with have never modeled this curve.

They look at the API pricing page, multiply by their POC volume, and call it a budget. Then they're shocked when the production bill arrives.

The math isn't complicated. It's just uncomfortable.

Token costs per request, times requests per user, times your user count. Add embedding generation, vector database hosting, compute overhead, and operational monitoring. Run that at your current scale. Then at 10x. Then at 100x.

If the unit economics don't work at 10x, you don't have a scaling problem. You have an architecture problem. And it's much cheaper to discover that before you've built the system.

Cost modeling isn't optional for AI features. It's foundational.

The companies getting this right are doing three things: modeling costs across scale scenarios before committing, designing cost guardrails (token budgets, caching, tiered processing) into the architecture from day one, and setting clear go/no-go thresholds tied to unit economics.

That's not conservative. It's just good engineering.

## 2025-12-17 — Multi-Tenancy Maturity

**Status:** published · **Topic:** M&A / Due Diligence

Multi-Tenancy Maturity: The Architecture That Makes or Breaks Enterprise SaaS Deals

That B2B SaaS platform you're acquiring handles 200 customers today. But can it handle 20 enterprise customers demanding dedicated infrastructure, data isolation, and custom SLAs?

Multi-tenancy architecture is one of the most overlooked risks in technical due diligence, and one of the most expensive to fix post-close.

The Spectrum of Multi-Tenancy:

Shared Everything. One database, one app instance, tenant ID on every row. Cheapest to run, hardest to isolate.

Shared App, Separate Databases. Better isolation, but connection pooling and migrations become complex at scale.

Separate Deployments. Full isolation, but operational overhead explodes with each new customer.

Most SaaS companies start at "shared everything" and never evolve. That works, until an enterprise buyer demands SOC 2 attestation for their data only.

Red Flags During Diligence:

No tenant-level resource limits. Cross-tenant data leaks possible. Single shared database with no path to isolation. No per-tenant audit logging. "Noisy neighbor" performance complaints. Enterprise prospects lost because dedicated infrastructure isn't possible.

Questions to Ask:

Can you provision a fully isolated environment for a single customer today? How do you prevent one tenant's workload from impacting others? What's the data export/deletion process for a single tenant? Can you provide tenant-specific audit logs? What's the largest customer by data volume?

Why This Matters for Valuation:

Enterprise customers pay 5-10x more than SMBs, but they demand isolation, compliance evidence, and custom SLAs. If the architecture can't support them without a major rewrite, you're buying an SMB-only business with an SMB ceiling.

Retrofitting multi-tenancy into a shared-everything architecture typically costs $300K-$800K and takes 6-12 months. That's not a roadmap item. It's a purchase price adjustment.

Before you close, know whether you're buying a platform that can grow into enterprise, or one that's architecturally capped at mid-market.

## 2025-11-20 — The Data Gravity Test

**Status:** published · **Topic:** M&A / Data Integration

Integration debt kills M&A deals. Not because the code is broken, but because data flows that look simple on paper hide dependencies that break when you touch them.

The Data Gravity Test is a 48-hour technical diligence framework that surfaces integration risks before they become post-close problems.

The Four-Point Test:

1. Schema drift pulse: Sample production and downstream warehouse schemas to identify fields that change without notice.
2. Contract volatility scan: Diff the last 12 months of API spec changes against actual client-facing documentation.
3. Load pattern fingerprint: Replay logs to identify batch windows that will collide with the acquirer's reporting cadence.
4. Observability coverage: Map data lineage against metrics/alerts to reveal blind spots in critical flows.

Common Red Flags:

Silent schema mutations. Manual dependencies (ETL jobs that pause for human intervention). Shadow transformations (unowned dbt models that became critical). Documentation drift.

These issues become six-figure remediation plans post-close. Pre-close, they're negotiation leverage for purchase price adjustments, holdbacks, or escrow terms.

Quick Self-Assessment:

1. Inventory all upstream/downstream consumers of core entities.
2. Trace lineage with timestamps to find cron jobs and manual scripts.
3. Diff live API responses, DB schemas, and ETL configs against documentation.
4. Score risk by blast radius.

If you're evaluating a B2B SaaS platform and the integration story feels too clean, pull on the data gravity thread.

Don't confuse "it works" with "it scales."
